On lattes

Three dollars for water and milk and a small theatre of competence. A field guide to the modern café — and what, exactly, it is selling you when it sells you a coffee.

A
Ayadi Ghaith · writing from a café that does, in fact, charge extra for oat milk
Share Save

There is a particular small ritual that happens, several million times a day, in cities you have heard of and a few you haven't. A person walks into a room that smells deliberately of beans, says a word in Italian that they have agreed, by group consensus, to mispronounce, and hands over the rough cost of a paperback in exchange for a paper cup of warm, slightly bitter liquid. They then sit down, sometimes, and consider this a small victory. I include myself in the count.

I want to be careful here. This is not going to be the essay you are bracing for. I have no interest in the line, repeated to the point of muscle memory by certain financial gentlemen, that the coffee is what is keeping you out of the property market. The arithmetic is silly and the politics is sillier. What I am interested in is the thing — the actual transaction, what it contains, and why we keep coming back to it even when we know, in the dim back of our heads, that we are paying for something we cannot quite name.

The visible product

The visible product is, of course, a beverage. It is mostly milk. The milk is mostly water. The water is, in the worst cases, also mostly water. Onto this we lay a thin stratum of espresso, an ounce or two, brewed under pressure for somewhere between twenty and thirty seconds. A trained barista will tell you that the difference between a good shot and a bad one is the matter of about four seconds of extraction time, and they are correct, and almost nobody can taste it. This is fine. The shot is not, mostly, what you are buying.

The café is the rare commercial space whose explicit product is consumed in roughly four minutes and whose actual product takes about forty. — a friend, paraphrased, over the second of two

What you are buying, instead, is a permission slip. You are buying twenty minutes of legitimate stillness in a city that mostly does not allow it. You are buying a chair that is not your chair, a table that is not your table, in a room that has agreed, by long convention, not to ask you to leave. The coffee is the cover charge.

The coffee is the cover charge. The chair is the show.

What the chair is doing

The chair is doing a lot. It is, for one thing, not the chair at your desk, which has, over the last three years, become a kind of accusation. It is also not the chair at home, where someone may, with great affection and at the wrong time, ask you a small honest question. It is the third chair: the one in Ray Oldenburg's sense, the one that doesn't belong to anyone in particular, and therefore briefly belongs to you.

The barista — and I have, in another life, been one — understands this perfectly. The good ones do a small choreography of unobtrusiveness, refill the water without being asked, and, crucially, never make eye contact when you are obviously crying into a laptop. This last skill is undervalued and, in my experience, the best baristas have it in the way that good bartenders do: as a professional kindness offered without comment.

The economics, briefly

The unit economics of a small café are, famously, terrible. The margin on a single drink, once you account for the beans, the milk, the cup, the lid, the rent, the wages, the electricity, the slow attrition of every espresso machine ever built — is somewhere between sad and worse. The bakery case carries the room. The cold-brew bottles in the fridge carry the bakery case. The good café knows this and accepts it; the bad café tries to make up the difference by becoming, slowly and against its will, a co-working space.

  1. The single best decision a small café can make is to refuse, politely and from the beginning, to install a printer.
  2. The second-best decision is to put the power outlets only on the back wall.
  3. The third is to have at least one table that is, by design, a little too small for a laptop and just right for a saucer.

None of this is mysterious to anyone who has spent five minutes thinking about it, and yet the small café persists. It persists because the alternative is worse. The alternative is a slightly cheaper drink, made slightly faster, served by a slightly less attentive person, in a slightly larger room owned by a slightly larger company. You can have that. It is, in fact, on most corners. You will notice that, when given a choice, most of us cross the street.

Why we cross the street

We cross the street because we are, in a small and recoverable way, lonely. Not lonely in the dramatic sense — the sense the magazines keep diagnosing and the apps keep monetizing — but lonely in the ordinary one, which is the condition of having a long quiet day and no obvious place to put yourself. The café is one of the few remaining institutions in a contemporary city that will sell you a brief solution to this problem, without comment, for under five dollars. Considered that way, it is the bargain of the century.

· · ·

It is now, by the clock, the second hour I have been sitting here, and the barista has refilled my water twice without saying anything, and a man at the next table has been on the same paragraph of the same novel for forty-five minutes, and the espresso machine has hissed at intervals like a small contented animal. I have, in that time, paid for one drink. I will, in a minute, pay for another, and I will leave a tip that is roughly the cost of the drink itself, because I am not, in the end, an idiot, and because I know exactly what I have been buying.

It was never the latte.

Notes

  1. The "third place" framing is borrowed wholesale from Oldenburg's The Great Good Place (1989), which is, if anything, more relevant now than it was then. Worth the afternoon.
  2. I am aware that some of the points above are made annually, by other people, in other publications. I do not claim novelty. I claim only that the chair is still, in my opinion, the show.
  3. The barista, asked to comment on this piece, declined politely, and then refilled the water.
